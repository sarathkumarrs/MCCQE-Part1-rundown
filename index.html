Your complete walkthrough of the MCCQE Part 1

The Medical Council of Canada Qualifying Examination Part 1 is the national exam that assesses whether you have the medical knowledge and clinical reasoning expected of a graduating Canadian medical student — and it's the first major gate for international medical graduates (IMGs) aiming to practise in Canada.

~230 MCQs 2 sections × 115 ≈6.5 hrs appointment Pass 439 / 600 No negative marking

01 · About the exam

A brief on the MCCQE Part 1

One computer-based, one-day exam delivered at Prometric test centres worldwide or by remote proctoring. It's a prerequisite for the Licentiate of the Medical Council of Canada (LMCC), which most provincial regulators require for licensure.

Who takes it

CMGs & IMGs

Canadian/US medical graduates take it near graduation. IMGs take it to enter the Canadian pathway — typically before or alongside the NAC OSCE and the CaRMS residency match.

What it tests

Knowledge + reasoning

Clinical knowledge and decision-making at the level of a graduating medical student, built on the official MCC Objectives — the definitive syllabus for the exam.

What changed

2025 refresh

Since April 2025 the Clinical Decision-Making (CDM) written cases are gone. The exam is now 100% multiple-choice and the appointment shrank from ~9 hours to ~6.5 hours. Ignore any prep material that still mentions CDM.

Registration: apply through your physiciansapply.ca account. The application fee is approximately CAD 1,500 and non-refundable, so book only when your preparation timeline is realistic. Sessions run a few times per year; scheduling opens about 5 months before each window.

Note on "marks": the MCCQE doesn't report marks out of 230. Your raw answers are converted to a scaled score between 300 and 600 that adjusts for question difficulty. Passing (439) corresponds to answering well over half the questions correctly — but there is no fixed percentage cut-off.

04 · Blueprint

Focus topics & exam blueprint

The MCCQE Part 1 is built on a two-axis blueprint. Every question is classified by a Dimension of Care and a Physician Activity, drawn from the official MCC Objectives.

Axis 1 · Dimensions of Care
  • Health promotion & illness prevention ~22%
  • Acute care ~34%
  • Chronic care ~31%
  • Psychosocial aspects of care ~13%
Axis 2 · Physician Activities
  • Assessment / diagnosis ~54%
  • Management ~32%
  • Communication ~7%
  • Professional behaviours ~7%

Blueprint weightings are approximate targets published by the MCC and can shift slightly between forms — always confirm against the current MCC blueprint.

The largest clinical domain. High-yield areas:
  • Cardiology — ACS, heart failure, arrhythmias, hypertension, ECG interpretation
  • Respirology — asthma, COPD, pneumonia, PE, pleural disease
  • Endocrinology — diabetes and its emergencies, thyroid, adrenal disorders
  • Nephrology, GI/hepatology, hematology, infectious disease, rheumatology, neurology
  • Interpreting labs, imaging and ECGs embedded in question stems
  • Acute abdomen — appendicitis, cholecystitis, bowel obstruction, pancreatitis
  • Trauma principles (ATLS approach), pre- and post-operative care
  • Vascular emergencies, hernias, breast and thyroid lumps
  • Urology and orthopedic basics, common fractures
  • Growth, development and immunization schedules (Canadian)
  • Neonatal jaundice, respiratory distress, febrile infant work-up
  • Common infections, asthma, bronchiolitis, gastroenteritis and dehydration
  • Child maltreatment recognition and mandatory reporting
  • Antenatal care, screening, hypertensive disorders of pregnancy
  • Labour and delivery, postpartum hemorrhage, obstetric emergencies
  • Contraception, abnormal uterine bleeding, menopause
  • Cervical screening and gynecologic cancers
  • Mood, anxiety, psychotic and substance-use disorders
  • Suicide risk assessment and emergency psychiatry
  • Delirium vs dementia, capacity and consent
  • Pharmacology of antidepressants, antipsychotics, lithium
A distinctly Canadian, heavily tested domain that IMGs often underestimate:
  • Epidemiology & biostatistics — sensitivity/specificity, study design, screening criteria
  • Consent, capacity, confidentiality and disclosure in the Canadian context
  • Public health — reportable diseases, outbreak response, immunization policy
  • The Canadian health system, patient safety and quality improvement
  • Indigenous health and culturally safe care
  • Periodic health exams and Canadian screening guidelines (breast, cervical, colorectal, lipids)
  • Counselling — smoking cessation, alcohol, obesity, safe driving
  • Undifferentiated presentations: fatigue, dizziness, headache, back pain
  • Geriatrics — falls, polypharmacy, frailty
Study anchor: the free, official MCC Objectives define every testable presentation. Map your question-bank practice (e.g., Canadian-focused banks) to those objectives rather than studying from USMLE-shaped material alone — the Canadian guidelines and PHELO content are where most IMG marks are lost.

05 · The pathway

Steps after passing the MCCQE Part 1

Passing is the entry ticket, not the destination. Here is the typical IMG sequence from a pass result to practising in Canada.

1

Verify credentials on physiciansapply.ca

Source-verify your medical degree and documents through the MCC's Physician Credentials Repository. Most downstream applications (CaRMS, provincial regulators) rely on this account.

2

Take the NAC OSCE

The National Assessment Collaboration OSCE is a clinical skills exam required of IMGs applying to Canadian residency. It's offered only within Canada, so plan travel and timing around CaRMS deadlines.

IMG-specific
3

Apply through CaRMS for residency

The Canadian Resident Matching Service runs the national match. IMG-designated seats are limited and often carry a return-of-service commitment to the sponsoring province. Language proficiency (IELTS/CELPIP or French equivalents) and strong references matter as much as scores.

4

Complete residency in Canada

Residents are employed and paid — you enter Canada on a work permit tied to your residency position, not as a student or economic-class immigrant. Family medicine is 2 years; specialties run 4–5+.

You are physically in Canada from this step
5

Earn the LMCC

With a pass on the MCCQE Part 1 plus at least 12 months of acceptable postgraduate training, you become eligible for the Licentiate of the Medical Council of Canada — the credential provincial regulators look for.

6

Certify and get licensed

Pass your certification exam — CCFP (family medicine) or Royal College (specialties) — then apply to the provincial College (e.g., CPSO in Ontario, CPSBC in British Columbia) for full, independent licensure.

7

Transition to permanent residence

Once working as a resident or physician in Canada, provincial nomination and other employer-driven routes (next section) can carry you to PR without ever entering the Express Entry pool.

06 · Immigration

Landing in Canada — beyond Express Entry

Express Entry is only one door. For physicians, several pathways lead to Canada — and to permanent residence — through healthcare demand rather than CRS points.

Most common for new IMGs

Residency work permit → PR

Matching through CaRMS gets you an employer-specific work permit as a medical resident. Time worked in Canada then unlocks provincial nomination or other in-Canada PR routes.

  • No Express Entry pool or CRS score involved to enter Canada
  • Return-of-service terms often pre-arrange your first job
For experienced physicians

Practice-Ready Assessment (PRA)

Provinces including BC, Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba and Nova Scotia run PRA programs that assess internationally experienced family physicians over ~12 weeks of supervised practice — bypassing residency entirely.

  • Requires MCCQE Part 1 (often NAC too) and recent independent practice
  • Leads to provisional licence + return-of-service in an underserved community
Province-driven PR

Provincial Nominee Programs — healthcare streams

Most provinces run physician or healthcare-professional PNP streams (e.g., BC PNP Healthcare Professional, OINP, SINP, Nova Scotia's Physician stream) that nominate you for PR based on a job offer from a health authority.

  • Nomination = a direct PR application, no CRS competition in base streams
  • Demand for physicians keeps these streams comparatively accessible
Atlantic provinces

Atlantic Immigration Program (AIP)

An employer-driven PR pathway for Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, PEI and Newfoundland & Labrador. A job offer from a designated employer — health authorities included — anchors the application.

  • Popular with physicians recruited to Atlantic Canada's shortage areas
  • Runs entirely outside the Express Entry pool
Verify before you act: immigration criteria, PRA availability and PNP streams change frequently and vary by province. Confirm current rules on the official MCC, CaRMS, provincial College and IRCC websites, or with a licensed immigration consultant, before committing money or timelines.
